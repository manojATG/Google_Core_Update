AI SEO Audit Compliance Requirements

Apply these rules to every tour page before publishing.

Why this matters

The audit showed that the tour page structure is strong, but AI readiness improves when the page has a concise meta description, clean heading hierarchy, a 30-50 word answer target after the H1, visible author bio, schema, citations, expert/source quotes, original route analysis, and topic-focused internal links.

✅ Must include on every page

  • Meta description around 140-155 characters.
  • Exactly one H1.
  • First paragraph after H1 must be 30-50 words and answer the page topic directly.
  • Logical headings only: H1, then H2, then H3. Do not jump from H2 to H4.
  • FAQPage schema for FAQ sections.
  • HowTo schema when app setup steps are listed.
  • Article schema linked to a Person or team author schema.
  • Visible author bio with a link to an author/about page.
  • Clear citations using “Source: ...” near factual claims.
  • At least one official-source quote or route-team quote where useful.
  • Original route analysis based on ATG/GA route data, tour script, app data, or customer-support patterns.
  • Topic-focused internal links using destination-specific anchor text.
  • Deploy llms.txt at the domain root.

🚫 Never do this

  • Do not leave meta description blank or overlong.
  • Do not use decorative headings that break hierarchy.
  • Do not rely only on tables without schema.
  • Do not cite vague “official sources” without naming them.
  • Do not use generic footer links as the main internal-link strategy.
  • Do not claim original research unless the page includes actual ATG/GA route insights, support feedback, app data, or tour-script analysis.
Technical note for dev: Add or update FAQPage, HowTo, Product, Article, Person/Organization, Breadcrumb, and Review schema where the visible page supports them. Also add a root-level llms.txt file for the domain.

SEO Title, Meta, URL, Heading Rules & Schema Setup

Content team provides inputs; SEO/dev implements technically.

Why this matters

This is the search and machine-readable foundation. The title and meta affect search-result clicks; headings help Google, LLMs, and users understand the page; schema helps machines read product, offer, FAQ, review, and breadcrumb information. Ushma/content team does not need to code schema. They only need to give accurate inputs that match the visible page.

✅ Must include

  • One H1 only: destination + tour/audio tour.
  • Title under 60 characters where possible.
  • Meta around 140–155 characters and specific to the actual page content.
  • Provide primary and secondary keywords, price, rating, review count, duration, distance, starting points, and tour type.
  • Schema must match visible page content: Product, FAQPage, BreadcrumbList, Review, Article/Person, and HowTo where applicable.
  • Meta description must be around 140-155 characters, not blank or overlong.
  • The first paragraph immediately after the H1 must be a 30-50 word answer target.
  • Heading hierarchy must not skip levels: H1 > H2 > H3 only.
  • FAQPage, HowTo, Article, Person/Organization, Product, Breadcrumb, and Review schema should be implemented where supported by visible content.

🚫 Never do this

  • Do not start titles with generic self-praise such as Best or Top unless supported by real objective proof.
  • Do not write a meta description that could apply to any destination.
  • Do not mark up schema for reviews, price, ratings, FAQs, or author claims that are not visible and verifiable on the page.
Pro tip: Developer validates schema with Google Rich Results Test before publishing.

Use current website where available; verify freshness.

Why this matters

This is the trip-failure prevention block. It proves helpfulness and trust because it warns travelers about reservations, fees, safety rules, road closures, and no-signal areas before they buy or start the drive.

✅ Must include

  • Include official-source link or source name.

🚫 Never do this

  • Do not hide mandatory fees/reservations near the bottom.
  • Do not turn safety warnings into soft suggestions.

Example Copy — Road to Hana

Road to Hana — pre-trip guide copy

Before You Drive the Road to Hana

The Road to Hana is not a drive to rush. Even though the audio tour itself runs about 2–3 hours, most travelers should plan a full day because the route includes waterfalls, beaches, viewpoints, food stops, short hikes, narrow roads, one-lane bridges, and limited cell signal.

Download the Action Tour Guide app and the Road to Hana tour while you still have strong Wi‑Fi or cell service. If your tour has more than one starting point, download all available route versions before you begin. Once downloaded, the tour can use GPS to play stories automatically as you drive.

Start with a full tank of gas, bring water and snacks, and avoid beginning too late. Around 7 AM is a good target if you want enough time for waterfalls, photos, Black Sand Beach planning, food stops, and a relaxed return. Choose your top 5–7 must-see stops before leaving; trying to stop everywhere is the fastest way to make the day feel rushed.

Printable Road to Hana Trip Planner

  • Download the tour before leaving Wi‑Fi.
  • Start around 7 AM.
  • Fill gas before starting.
  • Bring water, snacks, sunscreen, mosquito repellent, rain layer, water shoes, and comfortable walking shoes.
  • Reserve Waiʻānapanapa / Black Sand Beach if visiting.
  • Choose your top 5–7 stops before leaving.
  • Check weather and road conditions.
  • Connect phone to car speakers before driving.
  • Do not stop where parking is unsafe or not allowed.

Example Copy — Yellowstone

Yellowstone — pre-trip guide copy

Before You Drive Yellowstone

Yellowstone is not a single-stop park. The Grand Loop covers long distances, and driving between entrances, geyser basins, wildlife valleys, canyon overlooks, and lake areas can take hours. Plan your day around one region or one loop segment instead of trying to see the entire park in a single rushed day.

Download the Action Tour Guide app and Yellowstone tour before entering the park. Cell service is unreliable inside Yellowstone, especially away from developed villages and entrance areas. Once downloaded, GPS-triggered audio can play as you drive without relying on cellular data.

Bring binoculars for wildlife, layers for cold mornings, water, snacks, and a full fuel tank before long stretches. If you plan to hike, carry bear spray and know how to use it. Stay on boardwalks in thermal areas, and keep the required distance from wildlife.

Printable Yellowstone Trip Planner

  • Download the tour before entering the park.
  • Check NPS road status for your entrance.
  • Buy or prepare your entrance pass.
  • Pick one route focus: Old Faithful/Upper Geyser Basin, Lamar Valley, Canyon, Lake, or Mammoth.
  • Bring binoculars, layers, water, snacks, and sun protection.
  • Carry bear spray if hiking.
  • Stay 100 yards from bears/wolves and 25 yards from bison/elk.
  • Stay on boardwalks in thermal areas.
  • Plan extra time for wildlife jams and construction delays.

Key Road to Hana Stops

Twin Falls

Twin Falls is one of the first major waterfall stops on the Road to Hana, so it can get busy early. It works well as a short first stop, especially if you started near Kahului, but do not spend too long here if you also plan to reach Waiʻānapanapa, Wailua Falls, or Kīpahulu later in the day.

Audio value: The narration helps explain why this area marks the transition into the wetter, greener Hana Highway landscape.

Rainbow Eucalyptus Grove

The Rainbow Eucalyptus grove is one of the most recognizable sights along Hana Highway. The colorful bark makes it a popular photo stop, but visitors should only pull over where it is safe and legal.

Field note: This is better as a short visual stop than a long break. Do not block the road for photos.

Audio value: The narration explains why the bark has its multicolored, painted appearance.

Waiʻānapanapa State Park

Waiʻānapanapa is one of the most popular stops on Road to Hana because of its black sand beach, coastal scenery, sea stacks, lava formations, and cultural history. It is also one of the stops that requires the most planning.

Field note: Check reservation requirements before starting the drive. Do not assume same-day entry is available.

Audio value: The narration gives context before arrival so travelers understand why this stop is culturally and geologically significant.

Example Copy — Yellowstone

Yellowstone — key stops copy

Key Yellowstone Grand Loop Stops

Grand Prismatic Spring

Grand Prismatic Spring is Yellowstone’s most photographed thermal feature and one of the most recognizable sights in the park. The boardwalk gives a close view of the color bands, while the Fairy Falls overlook gives the wider perspective many visitors expect from photos.

Field note: The boardwalk is busiest from late morning through mid-afternoon. Steam can make photos difficult on cold mornings.

Audio value: The narration explains why the colors change from blue to green, yellow, orange, and red.

Lamar Valley

Lamar Valley is Yellowstone’s strongest wildlife corridor and one of the best places in the park to look for bison, pronghorn, bears, and wolves. Sightings are never guaranteed, but dawn and dusk give the best odds.

Field note: Bring binoculars and scan hillsides and river edges from pullouts. Do not stop in the road or approach wildlife.

Audio value: The narration explains what behavior to look for and why wildlife gathers in this valley.

Upper Geyser Basin and Old Faithful

Old Faithful is the headline stop, but Upper Geyser Basin contains far more than one geyser. Beehive, Grand, Castle, Riverside, and other geysers may erupt on less predictable schedules.

Field note: Check the NPS prediction board or NPS app when you arrive and give yourself time to walk the boardwalk loop.

Audio value: The narration explains why geysers erupt and why Old Faithful is regular enough to predict.

Example Copy — Road to Hana

Road to Hana — destination-specific section

Waiʻānapanapa Black Sand Beach Reservation Guide

Waiʻānapanapa State Park is one of the most popular Road to Hana stops, but visitors should not treat it as a casual drive-up stop. Non-resident visitors need advance reservations for entry, and visitors arriving by car must also plan for parking. Because the park is far along the route, choose a time slot that matches your realistic driving pace and planned stops.

Do not book a time slot that forces you to rush the first half of the drive. If you start from Kahului or Paʻia and stop often, it can take much longer than expected to reach mile marker 32. Download your reservation confirmation before leaving reliable signal.

If reservations are sold out, do not build your day around entering the park. Focus instead on stops such as Twin Falls, Keʻanae Peninsula, Wailua Falls, Hana Bay, or other accessible pullouts and viewpoints.

Example Copy — Yellowstone

Yellowstone — destination-specific section

Old Faithful Timing and Upper Geyser Basin Planning

Old Faithful is famous for predictable eruptions, but the shortcut phrase “every 90 minutes” is not precise enough for planning. The interval varies based on the length of the previous eruption. When you arrive, check the NPS prediction board or the NPS app and plan to be in viewing position about 15 minutes before the predicted eruption time.

Do not leave immediately after Old Faithful. Upper Geyser Basin contains the highest concentration of geysers in the park, and several other geysers may erupt during your visit. If you have time, walk part of the boardwalk loop and use the audio tour to understand what makes this basin different from Yellowstone’s other thermal areas.

Planning note: Peak crowds build from mid-morning through mid-afternoon. Early morning or late afternoon can make the experience easier.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a reservation to enter Yellowstone?
No general vehicle reservation or timed-entry reservation is required for standard Yellowstone entry. You still need a park entrance pass, and some activities require separate reservations or permits.

Does the Yellowstone audio tour work without cell service?
Yes. Download the tour before entering the park. After download, GPS-triggered audio can work offline.

Is the $35 park entrance fee included?
No. The park entrance pass is separate from the audio tour. A private vehicle entrance pass is valid for seven consecutive days and covers one private vehicle and its passengers.

How close can I get to Yellowstone wildlife?
Stay at least 100 yards from bears and wolves, and at least 25 yards from bison, elk, and other wildlife.

Which Yellowstone entrance should I use?
Use the entrance that fits your lodging and route plan. The tour covers all 5 entrances, but seasonal road access can change, so check NPS road status before traveling.
