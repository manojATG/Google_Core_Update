Critical Pre-Trip Alert

Important before you drive Road to Hana

Two things can disrupt the day more than anything else: Black Sand Beach reservations and road conditions.

Book before you drive
Waiʻānapanapa State Park, home to Maui’s famous Black Sand Beach, requires reservations. Non-resident visitors should reserve entry and parking in advance through the official Hawaiʻi State Parks reservation system. Do not wait until you are on Hana Highway because cell signal can be unreliable.

Source: Hawaiʻi DLNR Waiʻānapanapa FAQ.

Black Sand Beach reservation

Waiʻānapanapa State Park is located far along the route near mile marker 32. If this stop matters to your day, reserve it first and shape your driving pace around your time slot.

  • Check current hours, fees, and time slots.
  • Download your reservation voucher before leaving Wi-Fi.
  • Do not assume same-day entry or overflow parking is available.

Road and weather conditions

Hana Highway can be affected by weather, rockfall, fallen trees, local traffic restrictions, and roadwork. Check current conditions before leaving your resort.

  • Check Hawaii DOT roadwork notices.
  • Check Maui County alerts during storms.
  • Drive slowly on narrow roads and one-lane bridges.
Rental-car caution

Do not treat the back-side route as a simple shortcut

Remote stretches near Piʻilani Highway can be restricted by rental agreements, have limited signal, and may not include normal roadside assistance coverage. If your agreement does not clearly allow it, plan to return the way you came.

The Road to Hana is not a drive to rush. Even though the audio tour itself runs about 2–3 hours, most travelers should plan a full day because the route includes waterfalls, beaches, viewpoints, food stops, short hikes, narrow roads, one-lane bridges, and limited cell signal.

Before leaving, download the Action Tour Guide app and the Road to Hana tour while you still have strong Wi-Fi or cell service. Key Stops

Key Road to Hana stops covered by the audio tour

The tour covers far more than this short list, but these are the stops most travelers should understand before they drive.

Route insight
The best Road to Hana day is not about stopping everywhere. It is about choosing the stops that match your reservation time, energy level, road comfort, and return plan.
Early stop

Twin Falls

Twin Falls is one of the first major waterfall stops on the Road to Hana, so it can get busy early. It works well as a short first stop, especially if you started near Kahului, but do not spend too long here if you also plan to reach Waiʻānapanapa, Wailua Falls, or Kīpahulu later in the day.

Audio tour value: The narration helps frame the shift from normal Maui driving into the greener, wetter Hana Highway landscape.

Photo stop

Rainbow Eucalyptus

The Rainbow Eucalyptus grove is one of the most recognizable sights along the road. The colorful bark makes it a popular photo stop, but visitors should only pull over where it is safe and legal.

Practical note: Treat this as a short visual stop, not a long break. Do not block the road.

Short hike

Waikamoi Ridge Trail

Waikamoi Ridge Trail gives travelers a chance to step briefly into Maui’s lush forest instead of only viewing it through the windshield. It is best for travelers who want a short leg stretch without losing too much time.

Practical note: Conditions can be muddy. Wear shoes with grip if you plan to walk.

Food / cash

Nahiku Marketplace

Nahiku Marketplace can be a helpful mid-route food stop, but Road to Hana food stops can vary by day, hours, and cash/card availability. Do not rely on one stop as your only food plan.

Planning note: Pack snacks and water before leaving Paʻia.

Reservation

Waiʻānapanapa State Park

Waiʻānapanapa is one of the most popular Road to Hana stops because of its black sand beach, sea caves, blowholes, coastal trail, native hala forest, and volcanic coastline. It is also one of the stops that requires the most planning.

Practical note: Check reservation rules before starting the drive. Do not assume same-day entry is available.

Roadside view

Wailua Falls

Wailua Falls is one of the most dramatic roadside waterfall moments on the route. It is visible from the road, so it can be easier to enjoy than waterfall stops that require more walking.

Safety note: Pull over only where it is safe and legal. Do not stand in traffic lanes for photos.

Longer add-on

Pipiwai Trail

Pipiwai Trail is a major Kīpahulu-area highlight known for bamboo forest scenery and waterfall views. It is best for travelers who have started early, planned enough time, and are comfortable extending the day beyond Hana.

Planning note: This is not a quick photo stop. Budget real time if you include it.

Remote area

Kīpahulu Visitor Center

Kīpahulu is remote and far from most services. It can be rewarding, but travelers should arrive prepared with fuel, food, water, appropriate shoes, and a realistic return plan.

Official safety note: The National Park Service advises visitors to bring gas, food, water, sunscreen, mosquito repellent, and sturdy shoes for Kīpahulu.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Direct answers for the questions travelers ask before starting Road to Hana.

How many Road to Hana audio tours should I buy?

Buy one tour per car. Since this is a driving tour, everyone in the same vehicle can listen together through the car speakers.

What time should I leave for the Road to Hana?

Leave around 7 AM if you want time for waterfalls, viewpoints, food stops, photos, and a relaxed pace. Start earlier if you are coming from West Maui.

Does the tour work without cell service?

Yes. Download the tour before leaving strong Wi-Fi or cell signal. Once downloaded, the offline map and GPS-based audio can work in areas with limited signal.

Do I need a reservation for Black Sand Beach?

Check current Waiʻānapanapa State Park reservation rules before starting your drive. If reservations are required for your visit, book in advance because cell signal may be unreliable along Hana Highway.

Is Road to Hana dangerous?

Road to Hana requires careful driving because of narrow roads, curves, one-lane bridges, changing weather, and limited pull-off space. It is manageable for prepared drivers who drive slowly and avoid unsafe stops.

Can I drive Road to Hana by myself?

Yes. Many travelers drive Road to Hana themselves. Prepare by downloading your route, starting early, fueling up, bringing water and snacks, and checking current road and reservation information before you go.

Which direction is best?

Most visitors start from Kahului or Paʻia and drive toward Hana. Reverse or Kīpahulu-side starts should only be used if they fit your lodging, road conditions, and rental-car agreement.

Can I use CarPlay or Android Auto?

The current ATG page notes that CarPlay and Android Auto are not supported yet. Connect your phone to your car speakers through Bluetooth, USB, or Aux for audio playback.
