Tour Page Content Template

Every tour page across ATG and GuideAlong should follow this section order. Each section is here for a specific SEO reason — explained below. Use this as the brief when writing or briefing any tour page.

Must have — page will underperform without it
Should have — significant SEO and conversion value
Avoid — actively hurts rankings
Pro tip — small effort, outsized impact
Page Section Order — Top to Bottom
01
Page Title (HTML <title>) and Meta Description
Not visible on page — appears in Google search results
Must Have
Why This is the first thing Google and the searcher see. A weak title and meta means people don't click even if the page ranks. This is the fastest CTR improvement available — no ranking change needed.
✅ Title: Tour name + the most specific differentiator (e.g. "140+ Stories, All 5 Entrances") + brand name at the end
✅ Meta: Lead with the one thing that makes this tour different (GPS-triggered, works offline, specific stop count). Then name 2–3 pieces of practical content on the page (Black Sand Beach reservation guide, departure timing, Old Faithful eruption schedule). Under 155 characters.
✅ Keep title under 60 characters so it doesn't get cut off in search results
Example — Road to Hana
✓ Good: "Road to Hana Maui Audio Tour — Departure Guide, Black Sand Beach Reservations & GPS Stories | ATG"
✗ Avoid: "Best Road to Hana Maui Self Guided Driving Audio Tour" — no differentiators, "Best" is ignored by Google
Never do this
Don't start the title with "Best" or "Top" — Google discounts self-promotion superlatives. Don't write a meta that could apply to any tour ("Explore X with stunning views and expert narration"). Name the specific, practical content that's actually on the page.
02
Hero Section — Headline, Key Facts, Buy Button
Top of the page — first impression for both Google and the visitor
Must Have
Why Google reads the H1 (headline) to understand what the page is about. If your H1 doesn't clearly state the tour and destination, you lose ranking signal on every query that includes the destination name. The hero is also the conversion anchor — the buy button should be visible without scrolling.
✅ H1 headline: Must include the destination name and "tour" or "audio tour" — e.g. "Yellowstone National Park Audio Tour" or "Road to Hana Self-Guided Driving Tour"
✅ Subheadline: 1–2 sentences on what makes it different. Lead with function ("GPS-triggered stories that play automatically") not atmosphere ("Experience the magic of...")
✅ Fact pills / badges: Show the 4–6 most important tour specs at a glance: story count, offline capability, rating, purchase coverage (per car), expiry
✅ Buy button: Visible in the hero — price displayed clearly. "Buy This Tour — $9.99" is better than "Purchase Now"
✅ Rating display: Show star rating and review count in the hero. "4.8★ · 11,579 reviews" near the buy button
Never do this
Don't open with a quote, a poem, or atmosphere-only prose ("The road calls to those who dare to wander..."). Google can't index the intent. The visitor who found you via "road to hana tour" wants to confirm they're in the right place — tell them immediately.
Pro Tip
Add a sticky buy bar at the bottom of the page that follows the user as they scroll. This single element consistently improves conversion rates on long pages because the CTA stays visible during the entire reading experience.
03
Critical Pre-Trip Alert
Reservation requirements, safety warnings, permit rules — anything that causes a trip failure if missed
Must Have
Why Google's public guidance is broader: create helpful, reliable, people-first content that gives visitors a satisfying experience. For travel pages, the practical application is simple: prevent trip failures before they happen. A page that clearly explains mandatory reservations, seasonal closures, cell-service gaps, rental-car restrictions, and separate entry fees demonstrates useful route knowledge and helps the visitor even before they buy.
✅ Any mandatory reservation requirements (with the booking link and advance notice required)
✅ Safety rules with real consequences if broken (rental car prohibitions, park wildlife distance rules, towing risks)
✅ Download warning if cell service drops ("Download before you enter — no signal after Paia")
✅ Park entry fees that are separate from the tour cost (prevents refund requests from confused buyers)
✅ Official source checked — NPS, state park, recreation.gov, county road closure page, or the relevant attraction/operator page
✅ A visible "last verified" date for any reservation, fee, closure, or safety claim that can change
Example — what to include vs. what to skip
✓ "Wai'ānapanapa State Park (Black Sand Beach) requires reservations booked in advance at gostateparks.hawaii.gov — no same-day booking. Vehicles parked outside are towed."
✗ "Remember to pack sunscreen and comfortable shoes" — generic, zero E-E-A-T value, Google ignores it
Pro Tip
Style this as a visually distinct warning banner (yellow/amber background, warning icon) so it is impossible to miss. Safety and trip-critical details should not be buried near the bottom of the page.
03A
Source & Verification Block
Author, reviewer, route tester, last verified date, and official sources checked
Must Have
Why Travel pages contain details that change: fees, reservation rules, entrance access, road closures, cell-service gaps, and seasonal operating dates. A visible verification block makes the page more trustworthy for travelers and creates an internal accountability system for keeping critical facts current.
✅ Written by: ATG / GuideAlong Content Team, or named writer if available
✅ Reviewed by: Product/content reviewer responsible for factual accuracy
✅ Route tested by: Field tester, local reviewer, partner, or internal route owner — only include this if true
✅ Last verified: Month DD, YYYY — use this for reservation rules, fees, road status, seasonal access, and safety claims
✅ Sources checked: Official park/attraction page, state park page, recreation.gov, road closure page, or other primary source
Example — verification block
✓ Written by: ATG Content Team · Reviewed by: Route QA · Last verified: June 22, 2026 · Sources checked: NPS / State Park / Recreation.gov / official road alerts
✗ Avoid: "Route tested" or "last verified" claims that are copied across pages without a real check.
Never do this
Do not change dates just to make a page look fresh. Only update the verification date when a real check was completed and the source used is known.
04
Pre-Trip Planning Guide
Departure timing, logistics, what to bring — field-specific, not generic
Must Have
Why This is the section that proves the page understands the real trip, not just the destination. Generic packing lists ("bring snacks, water, sunscreen") have very low value because any competitor or AI tool can write them. Field-specific logistics ("leave West Maui by 7 AM or you'll hit parking gridlock at Twin Falls," "last fuel before Hana is in Paia") demonstrate direct route knowledge and answer planning queries the page may not currently capture.
✅ Departure timing — specific by departure location (not "leave early," but "leave by 7:30 AM from Kihei"). This is the most-searched pre-trip question for drive tours
✅ Fuel logistics — where is the last gas station before a long stretch? What does it cost? Are there stations inside the park/on the route?
✅ Food reality — not "you can find food along the way" but "the only food stop between X and Y is Z, cash only, sells out by 10 AM"
✅ App download instruction — explicitly: "download the tour at your hotel before you leave — cell service drops at [specific location] and you won't be able to download inside"
✅ What to actually pack — route-specific items only. Water shoes for volcanic rock waterfall pools, not just "comfortable shoes." Bear spray for Yellowstone. Binoculars for wildlife viewing.
Never do this
Do not copy-paste the same packing list across multiple tour pages. "Bring snacks, water, sunscreen, and comfortable shoes" appearing identically on your Road to Hana, Yellowstone, and Joshua Tree pages is a content duplication signal that drags all three pages down. Each tour's packing section must be specific to that route.
05
Key Stops Along the Route — with Field Notes
What the tour covers, stop by stop — with observations that prove firsthand knowledge
Must Have
Why Stop lists are common on every tour competitor's page. Field notes are not. This is where the page can show practical experience rather than generic destination coverage. A stop described as "Twin Falls — a beautiful waterfall with stunning views" is weak. "Twin Falls — arrive before 9 AM. The audio tells you which fork leads to the upper falls — most people take the wrong path and miss them" demonstrates route-specific observation. These field notes also capture long-tail planning queries such as "twin falls road to hana upper falls" and "which path at twin falls."
✅ List 6–10 key stops — not every stop, just the ones that need context or planning
✅ For each stop: What it is (1 sentence) + what to do there (1 sentence) + one field note — a specific observation that could only come from having been there
✅ Tag stops that require advance action (⚠ Reservation required, 💰 Cash only, 🅿 Limited parking — arrive before X)
✅ Note what the audio tour tells people at that stop — this is your product's unique value and should be referenced ("the audio explains why the bark looks this way," "the audio identifies which pullout to use")
Field note — good vs bad
✓ "Lamar Valley: go at dawn. The audio identifies which hillside to scan from each pullout — without this most visitors look in the wrong direction and miss the wolves entirely."
✗ "Lamar Valley: a beautiful valley known as America's Serengeti where you can see wildlife including wolves, bison, and bears." — Generic, any competitor can write this
06
Audio Sample — Transcript + How It Works
A real narration excerpt and a plain-language explanation of the app experience
Should Have
Why The audio tour is the product — but visitors can't hear it before buying. An actual narration transcript is the closest substitute and it serves two purposes: it demonstrates the quality and depth of storytelling (conversion), and it adds unique, indexable text content that no competitor has (SEO). A well-written narration excerpt also increases time-on-page significantly, which is a positive engagement signal.
✅ One real narration excerpt — 100–200 words, from one of the best stops on the tour. Should be genuinely interesting, not promotional
✅ Label it clearly as a sample ("Sample narration — plays automatically as you approach [stop name]")
✅ Follow with a 3–4 step "how it works" explanation: Download → GPS auto-plays → Stop whenever → Works offline
✅ Keep the "how it works" steps visual and scannable — this is a common question from buyers who aren't sure if the app will work without signal
Pro Tip
The narration excerpt is your single best conversion tool. Pick a stop that makes the reader think "I didn't know that" — something factually surprising that the visitor couldn't get from a Wikipedia article. The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction story and the Wai'ānapanapa black sand cave legend are examples of content that makes people want to hear the rest.
07
Tour Options Comparison Table
Honest comparison of audio tour vs. bus tour vs. private guide vs. DIY
Should Have
Why "Best [destination] tours" and "guided tours of [destination]" queries expect a comparison — not a single-brand recommendation. Pages that self-promote without comparing alternatives rank poorly for these queries. Adding an honest comparison table (where you're not the best at everything) actually improves both rankings and conversions, because it builds credibility. A visitor who trusts your honesty is more likely to buy from you.
✅ Compare 4 options: your audio tour, bus/group tour, private guided tour, driving without a guide
✅ Columns: Cost, Flexibility, Narration/Coverage, Best For
✅ Be honest — if a private guide is better for flexibility, say so. Your product wins on cost and convenience — let those advantages speak clearly
✅ "Best For" column is where you can guide — "independent travelers who want maximum depth at minimum cost" is a natural win for the audio tour
Never do this
Don't write a "comparison" where every row says your product wins. Google's quality raters specifically look for objectivity in comparison content — and visitors can see through it immediately. Giving a honest win to a competitor ("Private guide: best for special occasions and photography-focused visitors") makes your honest wins for the audio tour more credible.
08
FAQ — 6 to 8 Questions with Complete Answers
The most common pre-purchase questions — answered fully, not pointed to "contact us"
Must Have
Why FAQ sections help pages answer real pre-purchase questions in the same language travelers use in search. They can also support eligibility for FAQ-style rich results when implemented correctly, though Google does not guarantee rich-result display. More importantly, FAQ content reduces purchase friction by answering the questions that make people hesitate before buying. "Does it work without cell service?" and "How many people does one purchase cover?" are two common questions across both ATG and GuideAlong.
✅ Always include these 4 — universal across all tours:
→ Does the app work without cell service? (Yes — explain how: download first, GPS-only after)
→ How many people does one purchase cover? (One car — explain this clearly)
→ What is the park/site entry fee vs. the tour price? (Separate — prevents confusion and refund requests)
→ Does the tour expire? (No — valid forever)
✅ Then add 2–4 destination-specific questions — the top questions for that specific route (Black Sand Beach for Hana, Old Faithful timing for Yellowstone, etc.)
✅ Write complete answers — not "yes" or "contact us for details." A full answer to each question is what earns the featured snippet position
Never do this
Don't write FAQ answers that redirect to another page or say "download the app to find out." If the answer requires visiting another page, Google won't give you the featured snippet for that question — and a competitor who answers it fully will take it instead.
09
Reviews — Real, Specific, Sourced
3–6 verified reviews with attribution, not generic praise
Should Have
Why Reviews provide the social proof that converts hesitant buyers at the bottom of the page. More importantly for SEO, reviews contain natural language that matches how real travelers describe the product — "worked without signal," "the wolf viewing tip saved us," "banana bread sold out" — all of which can strengthen the page's relevance for long-tail search behavior. Reviews also build trust, especially when they are real, specific, dated, and sourced.
✅ Show the aggregate rating prominently above the reviews: "4.8 stars · 11,579 reviews on the Apple App Store"
✅ 3–6 reviews per page — each one should mention something specific about the destination, not just "great app!"
✅ Include the source (Apple App Store, Viator, Google) and a date — sourced reviews have more credibility than anonymous quotes
✅ Pick reviews that mention practical moments: a specific stop, something they learned, something the app warned them about. These are the review excerpts that help conversion
✅ Use only reviews that can be traced back to the source platform. Do not invent names, dates, ratings, or review text.
Never do this
Don't use generic reviews like "Amazing tour, highly recommend!" with no specifics. They add no SEO value and have almost no conversion impact. A review that says "The Old Faithful eruption timing in the app told us exactly when to get there and where to stand — we had the best view of anyone in the park" does 10x the conversion work.
10
Bundles & Upsells
Related tours, bundles, subscription — always last, never first
Should Have
Why Bundles placed at the bottom — after the helpful planning content — convert better because the visitor has already received value and understands the product. Bundles placed at the top before delivering any value can make the page feel sales-first and increase bounce risk. Putting upsells later keeps the page useful before it becomes commercial.
✅ Show 2–3 relevant bundle options — routes that most visitors combine with this one
✅ Show the "was" price and the bundle price — the saving should be visible and specific ("save $6.98")
✅ Add a one-sentence reason why these tours are usually combined ("Most Yellowstone visitors also visit Grand Teton — 1 hour south via the Rockefeller Parkway")
Never do this
Don't place the bundle upsell or Action+ subscription banner before the Pre-Trip Guide. Showing a subscription upsell before the visitor has received any value makes the page feel commercial before it feels helpful.
11
Visual Proof Layer — Images, Maps, Author Photos, App Screens
Use visuals to prove route experience and reduce planning uncertainty
Must Have
Why Long tour pages should not read like SEO documents. Images must prove firsthand route knowledge: maps, field photos, author/route-tester photos, app screenshots, parking proof, reservation proof, and stop thumbnails. Scenic images help emotion; field images help trust.
✅ Author / route tester image: real person, real team, or real field-testing photo. Do not use AI-generated people.
✅ Route map: simplified branded map showing start point, key stops, optional routes, and warning zones.
✅ Practical proof images: reservation screenshots, parking photos, no-cell-service visuals, road closure/entrance visuals, and app screenshots.
✅ Stop gallery: 6–10 licensed or owned images for the most important stops, each with useful caption and descriptive alt text.
Route map: show the real path, key stops, and risk points.
Route tester: connect the byline and verification block to a real person or team.
App screens: show download, offline map, auto-play, and stop list.
Never do this
Do not add random scenic stock photos just to make the page look rich. Every image should support the nearby section. Use descriptive filenames, real alt text, captions for practical images, and lazy loading below the fold.
AI SEO Auditor 100/100 Configuration Rules
AI
Machine-Readable Trust, Citations, and Topic Cluster Alignment
Use these rules when converting RTH and Yellowstone pages into trip-planner-style tour pages
Must Have
Why The RTH GA score improved only after the page had clean metadata, sequential headings, FAQ/HowTo/Article/Person schema, visible author bio, named-source quotes, explicit source citations, original route-analysis language, and a tightly aligned internal link cluster. Apply the same package to every priority tour page.

1. Metadata

  • Title: 50–60 characters, destination + tour type + brand.
  • Meta description: around 140–155 characters.
  • Use standard order: <meta name="description" content="...">.

2. Heading hierarchy

  • One H1 only.
  • Use H2 for major sections.
  • Use H3 for cards, FAQs, stops, and subsections. Avoid jumping from H2 to H4.

3. Schema pack

  • Product schema for price/rating/offer.
  • FAQPage schema for visible FAQs.
  • HowTo schema for app-use steps.
  • Article schema linked to Person author schema.
  • BreadcrumbList schema with topical breadcrumb labels.

4. Citation format

  • Every official fact should include visible (Source: URL).
  • Use named-source blockquotes with <cite>.
  • Do not use vague language like “official sources say” without attribution.

5. Originality signals

  • Include “Original route analysis” or “Proprietary route review.”
  • Mention app support patterns, route sequencing, field notes, or route-testing observations.
  • Do not make false route-testing claims.

6. Author bio visibility

  • Show an on-page Author Bio section.
  • Link to a detailed author page.
  • Use destination-specific author URL and anchor text.
Topical link rule
For high cluster alignment, avoid generic internal links such as Home, Privacy, Terms, Contact, or generic tour indexes on the page body/footer. Use destination-cluster links instead: Road to Hana pages should link to Maui/Road-to-Hana pages; Yellowstone pages should link to Yellowstone/Grand-Loop/Old-Faithful/entrance/road-status pages.

Technical Requirement — Do This on Every Tour Page
⚙
Structured Data (JSON-LD Schema)
One developer task per page — helps Google understand product, offer, review, FAQ, and breadcrumb details
Must Have
Why Structured data helps Google understand product details such as price, availability, rating, reviews, FAQs, and breadcrumbs. When implemented correctly and when the visible page content matches the markup, Product schema can make the page eligible for richer search results such as price and review information. It does not guarantee star ratings, rankings, or CTR improvement.
Product schema example — replace highlighted values and validate before publishing
<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Product",
  "name": "[Tour Name] — [Destination] Audio Tour",
  "description": "[1 sentence description]",
  "brand": { "@type": "Brand", "name": "Action Tour Guide" },
  "offers": {
    "@type": "Offer",
    "price": "9.99",
    "priceCurrency": "USD",
    "availability": "https://schema.org/InStock"
  },
  "aggregateRating": {
    "@type": "AggregateRating",
    "ratingValue": "4.8",
    "reviewCount": "11579",
    "bestRating": "5"
  }
}
</script>
Implementation Rule
Schema must match what is visibly shown on the page. Only use aggregateRating, reviewCount, price, and availability values that are accurate, current, and verifiable. Validate every page in Google's Rich Results Test before publishing.
Also add these schema types when present on the page
✅ FAQPage: only for FAQs that are visible on the page and written as full question/answer pairs
✅ BreadcrumbList: Home → Destination → Tour page
✅ Review: only if individual review text, source, rating, and date are visible and verifiable

Who · How · Why

Required Author, Reviewer & Method Information

Every published tour page should make it easy for visitors to understand who created the content, how the route information was checked, and why the page exists. This block should be adapted per destination and linked from the visible byline near the top of the page.

Who created it

Use a visible byline such as Action Tour Guide Content Team or GuideAlong Content Team. The byline should link to a short author profile explaining route expertise, editorial ownership, and review responsibility.

How it was verified

Show the route-tester or editorial review process: route driven or checked, official sources reviewed, app behavior verified, road/fee/reservation details fact-checked, and last material update recorded.

Why it exists

State that the page is built to help travelers plan and complete the trip safely and confidently, not merely to attract search traffic. If AI assistance is used in drafting, disclose that human editors verify all route, fee, safety, and reservation claims before publication.

Publishing rule: Do not publish a byline unless it links to supporting author/method information on the page or to a real author/editorial policy page. Do not add a “last verified” date unless the content has been materially checked against current route knowledge and official sources.
